Contraceptives, explained.

There are six common contraceptive methods. Here's how each one works, how long it lasts, and what to expect, side by side.

The methods

How the methods compare

These methods differ in how you use them and how much they ask of you day to day. Some use hormones, some don't. Tap Learn more on any method to see how it works.

A progestin injection given in the arm or buttock. Depo-Provera lasts three months; Sayana Press is a smaller version that can be given by a community health worker.

A pill taken at the same time each day. Brands like Microgynon combine estrogen and progestin; progestin-only "mini-pills" suit those who can't take estrogen.

A matchstick-sized rod placed under the skin of your upper arm. Jadelle lasts up to 5 years, Implanon NXT up to 3. The most effective method on this page.

Lasts up to 10–12 years

A small device a clinician places in your uterus. The copper IUD (such as the Copper T 380A) uses no hormones and can last over a decade. One of the most effective options there is.

A thin sheath worn during sex. Brands like Kiss, Fiesta and Protector Plus are sold cheaply almost everywhere. The only method here that also prevents HIV and other infections.

What to expect

Side effects

Most people adjust within a few months. Effects vary by method and by person.

Common & usually temporary

  • Spotting or irregular bleeding, especially in the first few months
  • Lighter, less frequent, or absent periods over time
  • Headaches, breast tenderness, or nausea early on
  • Mood changes or shifts in libido
  • Changes in appetite or weight for some methods

When to seek medical care

  • Severe leg pain or swelling, or chest pain and shortness of breath
  • A sudden, severe headache or changes in vision
  • Bleeding that's heavy and prolonged
